# Diabetes Management — A Simple Guide for Rural Patients

## What is Diabetes?

Diabetes (also called "sugar disease" or "madhumeh") is a condition where the sugar level in your blood becomes too high. When we eat food, our body breaks it down into sugar (glucose). This sugar gives energy to our body. But to move this sugar from the blood into the body's cells, we need something called "insulin." Insulin is made by a small organ near the stomach called the pancreas.

In diabetes, either the body does not make enough insulin, or the insulin it makes does not work properly. Because of this, sugar stays in the blood instead of going into the cells. Over time, too much sugar in the blood damages the eyes, kidneys, heart, nerves, and feet.

Think of insulin as a key that opens the door of your body's cells. If the key is missing or broken, sugar cannot enter the cells and just keeps building up in the blood.

Diabetes can happen to anyone — men, women, young or old. If someone in your family has diabetes, you are more likely to get it too. Being overweight, not exercising, and eating too much sweet or fried food also increases the risk.

## Symptoms — How to Know if You Have Diabetes

If you notice any of these problems, get your blood sugar tested:

- **Passing urine very often** — especially waking up many times at night to urinate
- **Feeling very thirsty all the time** — no matter how much water you drink
- **Feeling very hungry** — even after eating a full meal
- **Losing weight without trying** — your body is getting thinner even though you are eating
- **Feeling tired and weak all the time** — no energy to do daily work
- **Blurry vision** — things look unclear or foggy
- **Wounds that heal slowly** — any cut or sore takes a long time to get better
- **Tingling or numbness in hands and feet** — a pins-and-needles feeling
- **Getting infections again and again** — skin infections, gum problems, urinary infections

**Important:** Sometimes diabetes has no symptoms at all in the beginning. That is why everyone above 40 years should get their blood sugar checked every year. If someone in your family has diabetes, start checking from age 30.

## Diet — What to Eat and What to Avoid

Diet is the most important part of managing diabetes. The right food can keep your sugar under control.

### Foods You Should Eat:

- **Roti (chapati):** Wheat roti is fine, but eat only 2 at a time. Mixing barley flour (jau) or gram flour (besan) with wheat flour is even better.
- **Dal (lentils):** Moong dal, masoor dal, and arhar dal are all good. Dal has protein which is healthy. But use less ghee for tadka.
- **Vegetables:** Eat plenty of green vegetables — palak (spinach), methi (fenugreek), lauki (bottle gourd), turai (ridge gourd), bhindi (okra), karela (bitter gourd), baingan (eggplant). Karela is especially good for reducing blood sugar.
- **Salad:** Eat cucumber, tomato, radish, and carrot salad before every meal.
- **Fruits:** Apple, guava (amrud), orange (santra), papaya — but eat only one fruit at a time, in limited quantity.
- **Curd (dahi):** Plain curd without sugar is good for you.
- **Sprouted grains:** Sprouted moong and chana are very healthy.

### Foods to Avoid or Eat Less:

- **Rice (chawal):** Eat less white rice. If you must eat rice, have a small portion with dal and sabzi. Brown rice is a better choice.
- **Sugar:** Stop adding sugar to tea. Avoid mithai (sweets), gud (jaggery), and sugar completely.
- **Cold drinks and packaged juice:** These have a huge amount of sugar. Do not drink them at all.
- **Fried food:** Eat less puri, pakora, samosa, and other deep-fried items. Use less oil in cooking.
- **Maida (refined flour):** Avoid bread, biscuits, naan, and other maida items.
- **Potato (aloo):** Eat less potato. It has a lot of starch which raises blood sugar quickly.
- **Mango, banana, grapes, chikoo:** These fruits raise blood sugar fast. Avoid them.

### When and How to Eat:

- **Eat on time** — Have breakfast, lunch, and dinner at fixed times every day.
- **Eat small portions** — Do not eat too much at one time. Small meals are better.
- **Do not skip meals** — Skipping meals can make your sugar drop too low, which is dangerous.
- **Eat dinner early** — Finish your evening meal at least 2 hours before sleeping.

## Exercise — Keep Your Body Moving

Exercise is as important as medicine for diabetes. Regular exercise helps your body use sugar better and keeps blood sugar under control.

- **Walk for 30-45 minutes every day** — Walk briskly in the morning or evening. Walking in the fields or to the market also counts.
- **Do yoga** — Anulom-vilom (breathing exercise), kapalbhati, and mandukasana (frog pose) are very helpful for diabetes.
- **Ride a bicycle** — If you need to go somewhere nearby, take a cycle instead of a motorcycle or auto.
- **Do housework and farming** — Gardening, sweeping, cleaning, and farm work are all good physical activity.
- **Do not sit for too long** — Get up and move around every hour.

**Caution:** If your blood sugar is very high (above 300 mg/dL), avoid heavy exercise. Talk to your doctor first.

## Medicine — Take Your Medicine Regularly

- **Take the medicine your doctor has given you every day at the right time.** Do not miss even a single day.
- **Do not stop your medicine on your own** — Even if you feel fine, stopping medicine suddenly is dangerous.
- **Do not change the dose yourself** — Do not take more or less medicine without asking your doctor.
- **Insulin injection:** If your doctor has prescribed insulin, keep it in a cool place (fridge) and take it regularly as instructed.
- **Avoid quack doctors** — Do not take treatment from unqualified people. Always go to a government hospital or a registered doctor.
- **Do not rely only on home remedies** — Karela juice and jamun seeds may help, but do not stop your doctor's medicine for them.

## Checkups — Get Regular Blood Sugar Tests

- **Fasting blood sugar:** Blood test done in the morning on an empty stomach. Normal is below 100 mg/dL. If it is 126 or above, you have diabetes.
- **Post-meal sugar (PP):** Blood test done 2 hours after eating. Normal is below 140 mg/dL.
- **HbA1c test:** This test shows your average blood sugar over the past 3 months. It should be below 7%. Get this done every 3 months.
- **Check blood sugar at least once a month** — Your nearest Primary Health Centre (PHC) or government hospital offers free sugar testing.
- **Once a year:** Get your eyes checked, kidney test (urine test), and feet examined. Diabetes can damage these organs silently.

## Danger Signs — Go to the Hospital Immediately

If any of these happen, **go to the nearest hospital right away**. Do not wait:

- **Blood sugar goes very high** (above 300 mg/dL) and does not come down
- **Blood sugar drops very low** (below 70 mg/dL) — you may feel shaking, sweating, dizziness, or may faint. Immediately give one spoon of sugar or honey and rush to the hospital.
- **Becoming unconscious** or feeling extremely sleepy
- **Severe vomiting or diarrhea** that does not stop
- **Difficulty breathing**
- **Chest pain**
- **A wound on the foot** that is not healing or is turning black
- **High fever** that does not go down
- **Sudden loss of vision** in one or both eyes

**Remember:** Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) can be even more dangerous than high sugar. Always keep something sweet in your pocket — a toffee, sugar packet, or glucose biscuit.

## Government Help Available for You

The Indian government provides several facilities for diabetes patients:

- **Primary Health Centre (PHC):** Your nearest PHC offers free blood sugar testing and free diabetes medicine. Ask about the NCD (Non-Communicable Disease) clinic.
- **Ayushman Bharat — Health and Wellness Centres:** Village-level Health and Wellness Centres provide free diabetes screening and medicines.
- **Ayushman Bharat — PM-JAY Card:** If you have an Ayushman Bharat card, treatment for diabetes complications (kidney, eye, heart problems) can be covered up to Rs 5 lakh per year for free.
- **Jan Aushadhi Kendra:** These government medicine stores sell diabetes medicines at 50-90% less than market price. Metformin and other common diabetes drugs are available very cheaply here.
- **ASHA and ANM workers:** Contact your village ASHA didi or ANM for guidance. They can help you access government health facilities and schemes.
- **Health Helpline:** Call **104** for health-related information and guidance.

## Daily Routine — A Sample Day

| Time | What to Do |
|------|------------|
| 6:00 AM | Wake up, drink water, walk for 30 minutes or do yoga |
| 7:30 AM | Breakfast — 2 rotis + sabzi or daliya (porridge) + take morning medicine |
| 12:30 PM | Lunch — 2 rotis + dal + sabzi + salad |
| 4:00 PM | Light snack — roasted chana, peanuts, or one fruit |
| 7:00 PM | Dinner — 1-2 rotis + sabzi + plain dahi |
| 9:30 PM | Take night medicine (if prescribed), sleep on time |

---

**Remember:** Diabetes cannot be cured completely, but it can be controlled. With the right diet, regular exercise, timely medicine, and regular checkups — these four things — you can live a long and healthy life even with diabetes.

**Visit your nearest health centre today and get your blood sugar tested. Do not ignore diabetes — it can cause serious problems if left untreated.**
